Tips for better sleep

1. Be consistent

Go to bed at the same time each night and get up at the same time each morning, including on weekends and holidays. This helps set your body's internal clock and optimize the quality of your sleep. More precisely, fix a bed time when you normally feel tired, so that you don't toss and turn. If you're getting enough sleep, you should wake up naturally without an alarm. If you need an alarm clock, you may need an earlier bedtime.

2. Adjust you bedroom

Make sure your bedroom is quiet, dark, relaxing, and at a comfortable temperature. A relaxing environment is essential for a good night's rest. In fact, it is proven that people simply sleep better when their bedroom is optimized for light and noise levels, temperature, and comfort. It's also important to make sure that your bedroom is clean since a messy, cluttered bedroom can affect you more than you might think, especially when it comes to bedtime. To demonstrate, a study conducted by New York's St. Lawrence University, revealed that a messy bedroom can lead to a poor night's sleep and increased anxiety. Moreover, dust particles can make it harder to breath, and thus, affecting the quality of your sleep.

3. Avoid electronic devices

Phones, tablets, TVs, and other electronic devices can keep your brain awake due to the blue light, making it hard to truly wind down. In addition, the light from these devices can also suppress your natural production of melatonin (sleeping hormone). As much as possible, try to disconnect at least one hour before going to bed.

4. Be aware of what you consume

Avoid caffeine, and alcohol before bedtime. In addition, it is also important to avoid eating large meals at night because if your body is working to digest food, it's won't be able to get to a restful state as it would otherwise. As a result, you will have trouble falling asleep and the quality of your sleep will be impacted.

5. Be active during the day

Get some exercise. Being physically active during the day can help you fall asleep more easily at night. In fact, it's common to feel tired after a long or tough workout. In general, this occurs because your muscles run out of energy. Specifically, your central nervous system also loses its ability to keep moving your muscles. This causes muscle fatigue, making you feel tired and sleepy.
